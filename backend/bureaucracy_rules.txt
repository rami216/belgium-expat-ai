# BELGIAN EXPAT BUREAUCRACY SURVIVAL GUIDE: BRUSSELS EDITION

## 1. THE COMMUNE (TOWN HALL) & REGISTRATION
Rule: Every expat must register at their local commune (e.g., Molenbeek-Saint-Jean, Ixelles, Etterbeek) within 8 days of arriving in Belgium.
Process:
- Step 1: Book an appointment online or via email for "First Registration" (Première Inscription). 
- Step 2: Bring your Passport, Type D Visa, signed housing lease (contrat de bail), and Proof of Enrollment (e.g., from Vrije Universiteit Brussel).
- Step 3: You will receive an "Annex 15" (Annexe 15 / Bijlage 15). This is a temporary paper document proving you are in the registration process. It is NOT your final ID card.
- Step 4: The Police Check (Agent de Quartier). A local police officer will visit your apartment unannounced to verify you actually live there. Your name MUST be on the doorbell and mailbox. 
- Step 5: Once the police approve, the commune will summon you to order your actual Residence Permit (A-Card). This costs around 25-30 EUR and takes another 2-3 weeks to print.
Warning: Commune administration must legally be conducted in French or Dutch. They are not required to speak English. Bring a French-speaking friend or translate your documents beforehand.

## 2. BANKING & FINANCES
Rule: You need a bank account for rent, mobile plans, and daily life.
Traditional Banks: ING, KBC, and BNP Paribas Fortis are the main banks. However, they usually require your official Belgian ID card or Annex 15 to open an account. The process can be very slow.
Digital Banks: Revolut and Wise are highly recommended for your first few months. You can open them immediately with your passport, and they provide a European IBAN which is legally accepted for paying rent and bills in Belgium.
Student Visas & Blocked Accounts: Non-EU students must prove financial solvency to get or renew a Type D Visa. The safest way is using your university's Blocked Account procedure. You transfer the required yearly amount to the university, and they pay you a monthly allowance (e.g., around 800-1000 EUR/month) to your Belgian bank account.

## 3. HOUSING & LEASES (CONTRAT DE BAIL)
Rule: Finding a room (kot) or apartment requires specific legal paperwork. Rent for a student room is typically between 500 EUR and 700 EUR per month.
The Lease: The "contrat de bail" must be signed by both you and the landlord. 
Rental Deposit (Garantie Locative): By law, the deposit is usually 2 months of rent. NEVER transfer the deposit directly to the landlord's personal bank account. It must be placed in a special blocked bank account (compte bloqué) in your name.
Inventory (État des Lieux): Before moving in, an expert or the landlord will do a meticulous inspection of the property. Take photos of EVERYTHING. You will be charged for any scratches when you move out if they aren't documented here.

## 4. HEALTH INSURANCE (MUTUELLE)
Rule: Health insurance is mandatory in Belgium. 
Providers: You must register with a "Mutuelle" (e.g., Partenamut, CM, Solidaris). It costs around 10-15 EUR per month.
How the system works: Unlike some countries where insurance pays the hospital directly, in Belgium, you pay the doctor upfront. The doctor gives you a green paper receipt (Attestation de Soins). You drop this paper into your Mutuelle's mailbox, or scan it in their app, and they reimburse roughly 75-80% of the cost to your bank account a few days later.
Stickers (Vignettes): Your Mutuelle will mail you sheets of yellow or white stickers with your name and barcode. You must put a sticker on every medical receipt you send them.

## 5. PUBLIC TRANSPORT
Rule: Brussels has an excellent public transport network run by STIB/MIVB (Metro, Tram, Bus).
MOBIB Card: You cannot pay cash on the bus. You must buy a physical MOBIB card (costs 5 EUR) to hold your tickets or subscription. You can order it online or at a BOOTIK station.
Student Discount: If you are a student under 25, the yearly STIB subscription is heavily subsidized and costs only 12 EUR for the entire year. You need a school certificate to get this rate.
Trains: SNCB/NMBS runs the national trains (e.g., going from Brussels to Antwerp or Ghent). They offer a "Youth Ticket" for people under 26, which offers cheap flat-rate tickets anywhere in the country.

## 6. TELECOM & MOBILE NUMBERS
Rule: You need a +32 Belgian phone number for the commune, banking, and deliveries.
Providers: Mobile Vikings is very popular with expats and students because it's cheap, gives lots of data, and can be ordered entirely online. Other networks include Proximus, Orange, and Base.
Registration: By law, prepaid SIM cards cannot be anonymous. You will have to verify your identity with your passport or Belgian ID card to activate the SIM.
